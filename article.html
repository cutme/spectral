Selecting the optimum EDS detector for your application

By Eliot Tomac 23 August 2015

The development of silicon drift detectors have meant a big leap for microanalysis using
EDS in the SEM.

When you are looking for a new detector for energy dispersive X-ray microanalysis (EDS) there are several things to think about.

The introduction of silicon drift detectors has drastically improved the measurement speed by increasing the maximum counting speed compared to the previous liquid nitrogen cooled detectors. One effect of this is that typical EDS measurements now more include mapping and also enable advanced post processing.

Collection efficiency

Specially in applications when the number of X-ray photons are limited, for example when you measure beam sensitive materials like polymers or organic fibers, it is important to collect as many of the generated X-rays as possible. This is also true if you are planning to do EDS measurements at low accelerating voltages for improved lateral resolution or surface sensitivity. What is important in this case is to have a large solid angle.

Solid angle

The solid angle is defined by the detector area and the distance from the measurement spot on the sample to the detector. Important to remember is that as detectors gets larger collection area they often also get a larger physical diameter which will cause them to be positioned further away from the sample. So in order to know what solid angle you actually get with a specific detector you need to ask your supplier about the specific combination of SEM and detector model.

It is possible to allow the detector to reach below the pole piece of the SEM allowing for a very short distance to the sample. This however is not recommended by the microscope suppliers as it put the detector at risk for collision with the sample. But there are solutions that allow the detector to be moved down and in, like the Bruker VZ adapter or by having a sliding mechanism in the port adapter. This could allow an occasional very short distance for extreme measurements.

Converting photons into pulses

In the detector chip the incoming photon will be converted to an electrical charge which are collected at the anode aided by an electrical field, drift field, in the detector. The anode is connected to a field effect transistor, FET, placed either directly on the chip or attached to it. The FET converts the charge into a voltage output with is sent to the multichannel analyzer through a preamplifier and an analogue to digital converter, ADC.
